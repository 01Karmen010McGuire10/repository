Foreword

The production of this CEN Workshop Agreement (CWA) was formally accepted as part of the CEN/ISSS Workshop on Metadata for Multimedia Information - Dublin Core (WS/MMI-DC) in the Workshop's plenary meeting on 2004-01-18.

This CWA was agreed upon by the contributing partners in the CEN/ISSS Workshop on MMI-DC, representing a wide mix of interests, including administrations, libraries, on-line education and geographic information systems. The list of company individuals who have supported the document's contents may be obtained from the CEN/ISSS Secretariat.

 

Introduction

In the domain of government information, more public sector organisations in Europe are using Dublin Core as the basis for their information systems. In earlier communications with the MIReG activity in the IDA program where strategic directions and base specifications are being established, it was identified that there is a need for detailed implementation guidance to ensure compatible solutions in the various Member States and that MMI-DC would be the appropriate platform for this. This draft CWA builds on CWA 14860 "Dublin Core e-Government Application Profiles"

The work towards a common European metadata standard for e-Government is at its early stages. Therefore, this Draft CWA aims to provide the basis for that work and a first version of the metadata framework. In this respect this draft CWA should be considered a "live" document and, thus, should be regularly revised according to discussions between stakeholders and Member States.

There is a conflict, with a pan-European metadata framework agreement, in meeting the needs of different Member States who are at different stages of national metadata framework development. This draft CEN Workshop Agreement metadata framework is not intended to prevent established national frameworks from developing and implementing their own. This applies particularly to countries with advanced development in controlled vocabularies, as some public sector organisations may be required to implement them. A pan-European metadata framework should allow Member States to implement metadata advances while there is no pan-European taxonomy in place. A pan-European controlled vocabulary will need to take into account local controlled vocabularies.

When element sets are robust, they can then be applied to controlled vocabularies. In practical terms, a metadata registry functionality will be necessary, and will need to include encoding scheme names as well as element names.

European public bodies are by far the largest producers of information [5]. The European Commission with a recent directive aim at opening up public content for commercial exploitation [6]. Furthermore, the eEurope 2005 initiative suggests that e-Government should be a priority for Member States.

The use of metadata is an essential prerequisite for enabling citizens to discover and retrieve governmental resources and for businesses to exploit these resources. According to the draft "European Interoperability Framework for pan-European e-Government Services" by the European Commission [7], metadata is essential in achieving semantic interoperability of e-services at a European level. In the same report, it is recommended that "initiatives at pan-European level to develop common semantics on the basis of XML should be performed in a co-ordinated way, and should consider co-operation with the existing standardization bodies".

The Dublin Core Metadata Initiative is currently updating a simple, yet concise element set that can be used as a basis by different communities, according to their needs. In the domain of e-Government, different countries have specified and implemented their e-Government metadata standards based on Dublin Core. At the European level, the IDA MIReG project has proposed a European metadata standard and implemented a software tool that can be used by administrations for experimentation [8].

The work of the European Committee for Standardization aims to fill in the gap that exists at the European level for a simple e-Government Metadata Framework and supporting deployment guidelines. The aim of this framework in not to replace national initiatives but rather to complement them with a European dimension thus ensuring interoperability at a European level and beyond.

The model proposed is DC-centric, not because the universe revolves around Dublin Core but rather that other schema, such as geospatial, educational and imaging, are or have been crosswalked to Dublin Core but not the other way round. For example, following this framework in relationship to geospatial aspects will require a commitment to ISO 19115 Geographic Information – Metadata as it is set in the CWA crosswalk with Dublin Core listed in the body of this document.

There is a concentration on the management of element sets, rather than on the extensibility of vocabularies that requires rebalancing, but it is outside the scope of this framework to resolve vocabulary issues.

 


1 Scope

This Draft CEN Workshop Agreement presents an EU e-Government Metadata Framework. Guidelines for the deployment of the framework.

This Draft CEN Workshop Agreement is the result of co-operation between the members of the CEN/ISSS MMI-DC workshop from January 2004 to December 2004 and capitalises on work reported in CWA 14859 [1] and CWA 14860 [2].

This Draft CEN Workshop Agreement is applicable to three main categories of people:

  1. strategic decision makers in government/public sector

  2. policy makers in government/public sector

  3. metadata and content providers

Vendors of systems and software will not be addressed directly but it is conceivable that they will also benefit from the material.

This Draft CWA details the implementation framework to ensure compatible solutions in the various Member States.

This CEN Workshop Agreement endorses the Dublin Core metadata standard as the basis for metadata in e-Government.

This CEN Workshop Agreement endorses the extra elements found in MIReG.

Note: Dublin Core is well suited for its original discovery purposes. For a more extensive vision (such as that set out in the introduction: " achieving semantic interoperability of e-services"), Dublin Core and MIReG alone will not meet all e-Government needs.

This CEN Workshop Agreement endorses the CWA e-Government Metadata Application Profile.

This CEN Workshop Agreement recognizes the need to develop a pan-European e-Government Thesaurus but recognizes it is out of the scope of this CWA.

This Draft CWA takes into account the activities of IDA MIReG as well as of the DCMI Government Working Group.

This framework embraces the work Member States are involved in relating to metadata.

This framework will be compliant with other EU e-Government standards and policies, and will provide technology bridges between Member States’ needs and pan-European needs.

This framework will be compliant with maintained international standards and CEN standards.

3 A Hierarchical Metadata Framework

3.1 Overview

A hierarchal structure denotes one agreed standard at the European level "European Metadata Authority", and Member States’ "Metadata Authority MSx" standards at a second level. The requirement is for the Member States’ level to be synchronised with the European level at all times, thus allowing one Member State’s standards development to not necessarily be in step with another Member State’s standards development provided that the framework guidelines are followed.

The approach of a central authority, with co-ordinated ("synchronised") sub-authorities is a practical solution. However, the local governance arrangements may, in practice, be more complex than the current diagram indicates. There will also need to be an upwards co-ordination activity from the EMA to DCMI, especially, but also to governance bodies of other related standards.

3.2 Metadata Interoperability Interfaces

Communication between the European Authority and the Member States Authorities is performed using the "Metadata Interoperability Interfaces" in a direct analogy of the "Business Interoperability Interface" proposed in the "European Interoperability Framework for pan-European e-Government Services".

The same structure also indicates the relationship between different metadata standards. There is one European Metadata Standard that is maintained by the European Metadata Authority and national metadata standards that are maintained by the metadata authorities of the Member States.

The main requirement is that the national standards are always in "synchronisation" with the European standard. Thus, the national MII should include "technological bridges" that enable interoperability with the European standard and, through it, with other Member States.

In some Member States the process of specifying a metadata standard is still in its early stages. It is advised that these Member States directly adopt the European standard thus preserving resources and ensuring interoperability at a European level.

The use of the European Metadata Standard is not restricted to European Member States. Instead its potential use is also extended to EU Institutions including the European Commission. In that respect, all guidelines that are presented in this document for Member States are also valid for EU institutions. However, the term "Member States" will be maintained to better reflect the main audience of this draft CWA.

Note 1: It will be necessary to be able to extract EMA compliant metadata records from any Member States' compliant record. Furthermore, Member States' Authorities will not be able to make mandatory any element that does not have a similar obligation at the European level.

Note 2: In some cases Metadata Authorities may be operated by EU Institutions as well as states. Situations may arise where a national institution needs to comply with standards set by its national Metadata Authority and relevant EU (Sectoral) Authority.

3.3 Adoption of existing practices for interoperability

3.3.1 Overview

The proposed Metadata Framework will result in a number of e-Government services for use at pan-European level, such as discovery of governmental resources, searching and retrieval. These services, like any other e-Government service, should themselves respect the underlying principles proposed for all pan-European e-Government services in the "European Interoperability Framework for pan-European e-Government Services". These principles include: accessibility, multilingualism, security, privacy, subsidiarity, use of open standards, assessment of the benefits of open source software and use of multilateral solutions.

There are various practices that are followed by many Member States that are not Member State specific included in this framework. These are listed in this section, as adopting them will provide a sound basis for interoperability in a stable European Framework for e-Government.

 

3.3.2 IDA: "European Interoperability Framework"

The EIF focuses on supplementing, rather than replacing, national interoperability guidance by adding the pan-European dimension. This means that, if a Member State Administration wants to interoperate at the pan-European level, there should be in place a national interoperability framework, or equivalent technical strategy, to deliver e-Government services. The EIF can then provide the pan-European layer to the national framework.

3.3.3 ISO: "The Dublin Core metadata element set"

The Dublin Core metadata element set (ISO 15863) is a standard for cross-domain information resource description and was originally designed for resource discovery. It is probably the most widely used and recommended metadata set. In addition, many Member States have either mandated the Dublin Core as the standard for metadata in their public administrations or have already developed their own national metadata sets or variations of the Dublin Core.

3.3.4 IDA: "Management Information Resources for e-Government"

MIReG is a project that aims to develop extensions to the Dublin Core for government information, based primarily on the national metadata recommendations of the Member States’ public administrations.

The purpose of MIReG is to look at the national recommendations and other international recommendations for metadata for public sector information and, based on these, to develop extensions to the Dublin Core for government information.

MIReG is also concerned with tools for the management of metadata through the storage and retrieval of XML schemata.

The objective of this action is to produce a metadata framework for government information in pan-European applications, with associated vocabulary control, ontologies and topic maps, and best practice guidelines.

This document is intended as an entry point for users of Dublin Core. Specialists may find the document a useful point of reference to the documentation of Dublin Core as it changes and grows.

 

3.3.7 DCMI: "DCMI Government Working Group"

The Government working group is a forum for individuals involved in implementing Dublin Core within and between government agencies and International Governmental Organizations (IGOs).

The Working Group will identify commonalities in current public administration metadata implementations, and make recommendations, for example, for future DC qualifiers or extensions. The Working Group will co-operate with other international standardization initiatives, as appropriate, and when benefits for the working group can be readily identified.

 

3.3.8 ISO: "Information Retrieval – Z39.50"

IS 23950, "Information Retrieval; Application Service Definition and Protocol Specification, is a protocol which specifies data structures and interchange rules that allow a client machine to search databases on a server machine and retrieve records that are identified as a result of such a search.

 

3.3.9 ISO/IEC: "Information Technology - Metadata Registries (MDR)"

The ISO/IEC 11179: "Information Technology - Metadata Registries (MDR)" standard is a multipart standard composed of the following parts:

  1. Part 1: Framework

    2. introduces and discusses fundamental ideas of data elements, value domains, data element concepts, conceptual domains, and classification schemes essential to the understanding of this set of standards and provides the context for associating the individual parts of ISO/IEC 11179.

  2. Part 2: Classification

    3. provides a conceptual model for managing classification schemes. There are many structures used to organize classification schemes and there are many subject matter areas that classification schemes describe. So, this Part also provides a two-faceted classification for classification schemes themselves.

  3. Part 3: Registry Metamodel and Basic Attributes

    4. specifies a conceptual model for a metadata registry. It is limited to a set of basic attributes for data elements, data element concepts, value domains, conceptual domains, classification schemes, and other related classes, called administered items. The basic attributes specified for data elements in ISO/IEC 11179-3:1994 are provided in this revision.

  4. Part 4: Formulation of Data Definitions

    5. provides guidance on how to develop unambiguous data definitions. A number of specific rules and guidelines are presented in ISO/IEC 11179-4 that specify exactly how a data definition should be formed. A precise, well-formed definition is one of the most critical requirements for shared understanding of an administered item; well-formed definitions are imperative for the exchange of information. Only if every user has a common and exact understanding of the data item can it be exchanged trouble-free.

  5. Part 5: Naming and Identification Principles

    6. provides guidance for the identification of administered items. Identification is a broad term for designating, or identifying, a particular data item. Identification can be accomplished in various ways, depending upon the use of the identifier. Identification includes the assignment of numerical identifiers that have no inherent meanings to humans; icons (graphic symbols to which meaning has been assigned); and names with embedded meaning, usually for human understanding, that are associated with the data item's definition and value domain.

  6. Part 6: Registration

provides instruction on how a registration applicant may register a data item with a central Registration Authority and the allocation of unique identifiers for each data item. Maintenance of administered items already registered is also specified in this document.


3.4 Recommendations

The framework is complemented with a set of recommendations divided into organisational, technical and semantic:

Overall Recommendations:

  • Recommendation O1. (Overall – organisational)

A European Metadata Authority should be established. Member States should establish National Metadata Authorities, if they have not already. Member States to inform the European Authority of one National Authority responsible for Metadata.

Note: An agreement will be needed on how the interface between Member States' Metadata Authorities and those of EU Institutions will be managed.

  • Recommendation O2. (Overall – semantic)

The European Metadata Authority in collaboration with the Member States Metadata Authorities should adopt a set of rules for establishing, documenting and maintaining a European e-Government Metadata Standard.

  • Recommendation O3. (Overall – organisational)

The European Metadata Authority and the Member States’ Metadata Authorities should agree on the necessary Metadata Interoperability Interfaces through which their relevant business processes will be able to interoperate at pan-European level.

  • Recommendation O4. (Overall – technical)

The European Metadata Authority and the Member States’ Metadata Authorities should agree on the technical details and functionality of the supporting software, this includes specifying the provided services.

Recommendations for the European Authority:

  • Recommendation E1. (European Authority – semantic)

The Authority should adopt a set of rules for establishing, documenting and maintaining the European Metadata Standard.

  • Recommendation E2. (European Authority – organisational)

The Authority should establish the necessary Metadata Interoperability Interfaces to support the business processes agreed with the Member States’ Metadata Authorities.

  • Recommendation E3. (European Authority – technical)

  • The Authority should maintain proper implementation of all metadata terms in the European Metadata Standard in XML and/or RDF.

  • Recommendation E4. (European Authority – technical)

The Authority should implement and maintain a European Software Gateway that enables two-way communication between software components at MMI of the different Member States. This communication should support all agreed functionalities and should be fully multilingual.

Recommendations for the Member States Authorities:

  • Recommendation MS1. (MS Authority – semantic)

The Member States’ Authority is responsible for ensuring that the national standard is compliant to the European Standard at all times.

  • Recommendation MS2. (MS – organisational)

The Member States’ Authority should establish the necessary Metadata Interoperability Interfaces to support the business processes agreed with the European Metadata Authority.

  • Recommendation MS3. (MS – technical)

The Member States’ Authority should be responsible for the development and maintenance of a National Software Gateway that enables two-way communication between national and European Authority software components. This communication should support all agreed functionalities and should be fully multilingual.
